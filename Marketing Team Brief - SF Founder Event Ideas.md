## Executive Takeaway

The strongest event strategy is not a random social calendar. It should sell the Bay Area by letting founders feel three things directly:

1. **Professional density:** access to YC alumni, frontier operators, labs, early employees, and local technical communities.
2. **Emotional attachment:** nature, food, neighborhood texture, music, ferries, and shared memories that make SF feel like a life, not just a work location.
3. **Belonging:** repeatable rituals and small-group relationships that survive after the batch.

For Supabase, the highest-leverage posture is **quietly useful host and access layer**, not heavy corporate branding. Sponsor the moments where founders need hospitality, logistics, or access: reserved tables, meal buyouts, ferry coordination, private alumni dinners, developer salons, and post-event spaces.

## Critical Read of the Source Reports

The reports are directionally strong but over-inclusive. The 100-idea list captures the right emotional territory, but many ideas are too small, too touristy, or too logistically fragile to present as a serious marketing program. The best material clusters around a smaller portfolio:

- **Keep:** founder-alumni dinners, Angel Island, Golden Gate Park picnic, Mission murals and food, Chinatown banquet, Wave Organ/Crissy sunset, Stern Grove, Hardly Strictly-adjacent picnic, Fleet Week boat, makerspace/arcade, South Bay systems trek.
- **Use selectively:** Outside Lands, Portola, Giants suites, Dreamforce side events, hot springs/spa, private sailing. These can be expensive or feel generic unless Supabase adds a founder-specific reason to be there.
- **Deprioritize as standalone:** record store rambles, small hidden-gem walks, public chess/cardio events, generic hikes without a meal or alumni layer. They are good as passport items, not headline events.
- **Handle carefully:** Folsom Street Fair, alcohol-heavy brewery crawls, wellness events, and intense hikes. These should be opt-in and framed with context, accessibility, dietary, and non-drinker options.

## Marketing Positioning

### Recommended message

**"Build here because the work, the people, and the life compound together."**

### What Supabase should emphasize

- **Builder hospitality:** make it easier for founders to discover the city and meet the right people.
- **Open, local, and useful:** sponsor access and logistics rather than plastering the city with logos.
- **Community beyond the batch:** convert one-off events into rituals, passports, small groups, and alumni pathways.

### What to avoid

- Overproduced corporate offsites.
- Events that feel like recruiting or sales.
- Generic luxury without a founder-specific reason.
- Alcohol-only programming.
- Big public festivals without a hosted layer.
- Expensive events that displace the higher-value alumni dinner budget.

## Budget Guidance

| Program shape | Approx. cost | What it includes | Best for |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Lean pilot | $15k-$30k | Picnic, 2 alumni dinners, Mission tour, sunset ritual, one public event hosted layer | Testing resonance with one cohort |
| Strong batch program | $50k-$100k | 8-week calendar, several dinners, Angel Island, Chinatown banquet, makerspace night, one sponsored/partnered SF event | Serious YC + Supabase retention push |
| Premium flagship | $150k-$300k+ | Strong batch program plus sailing/Fleet Week/Outside Lands/Stern Grove reserved access and post-Demo Day celebration | High-profile launch or annual tradition |

## Measurement Plan

Track:
- Attendance and repeat participation.
- Founder satisfaction after each event.
- Number of meaningful new local connections per founder.
- Alumni participation rate.
- "Moment I felt I could live here" qualitative stories.
- Passport completion rate.
- Post-batch SF stay intention, then actual stay rate at 3, 6, and 12 months.

The key metric is not raw attendance. It is whether founders leave with local friends, routines, places, and alumni relationships they would miss if they moved away.
